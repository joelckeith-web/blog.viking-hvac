## Pre-Heat Wave AC System Inspection Checklist

Before temperatures soar, homeowners should conduct a thorough inspection of their cooling system. Start with your outdoor condenser unit – clear all vegetation, debris, and obstructions from around the unit, maintaining at least two feet of clearance on all sides. Check for bent fins, which reduce airflow and efficiency.

Indoor preparation includes replacing air filters, which should be changed every 60-90 days in Arizona's dusty conditions. During extreme heat preparation, install a fresh, high-quality filter to ensure optimal airflow when your system works hardest.

Inspect your thermostat settings and consider upgrading to a smart thermostat if you haven't already. Smart thermostats can provide 20-30% energy reduction through advanced scheduling and learning algorithms, particularly valuable during expensive peak cooling months.

Check all vents and returns for obstructions, ensuring furniture, curtains, or other items aren't blocking airflow. Poor airflow forces your system to work harder and can lead to frozen coils or compressor damage during extreme heat.

## Energy Efficiency Strategies for Extreme Heat Survival

During 107°F weather, energy efficiency becomes crucial for both cost control and system longevity. Set your thermostat to 78-80°F when home and 85°F when away – avoid drastic temperature adjustments that force your system to work harder than necessary.

Utilize ceiling fans to create air movement, allowing comfortable temperatures at higher thermostat settings. Fans use minimal electricity compared to air conditioning and can make 80°F feel like 76°F through wind chill effect.

Close blinds and curtains during peak sun hours to reduce solar heat gain. South and west-facing windows contribute significantly to indoor heat load, forcing your AC to work harder. Consider upgrading to energy-efficient window treatments or tinting.

According to the [Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/air-conditioning), proper insulation and air sealing can reduce cooling costs by 15-30%. During extreme heat, every degree of temperature infiltration increases system workload and energy consumption.

Time-of-use rate structures from local utilities SRP and APS make energy efficiency even more critical. Peak rates during afternoon hours can triple electricity costs, making efficient system operation essential for reasonable utility bills.

Consider upgrading to a high-efficiency system if your current unit is over 12 years old. Newer systems achieve SEER ratings of 16-20, compared to older units that may operate at SEER 8-10. The efficiency improvement becomes most valuable during extreme heat operation.

## Warning Signs Your AC Won't Survive the Heat Wave

Several warning signs indicate your air conditioning system may fail during extreme heat conditions. Reduced airflow from vents suggests filter blockages, ductwork problems, or failing blower components that will struggle under continuous operation demands.

Warm air from supply vents indicates refrigerant leaks, compressor problems, or electrical issues that require immediate professional attention. These problems worsen rapidly under extreme heat stress.

Unusual noises including grinding, squealing, or rattling sounds suggest mechanical problems that can cause complete system failure during peak demand periods. Don't ignore these warning signs hoping they'll resolve themselves.

Ice formation on indoor coils or refrigerant lines indicates airflow problems or refrigerant issues. Ice formation during extreme heat often signals impending system failure requiring emergency repair.

Frequent system cycling or inability to maintain desired temperatures suggests your system is undersized, inefficient, or experiencing mechanical problems. Systems struggling before extreme heat will likely fail completely when temperatures peak.

High energy bills without corresponding comfort improvements indicate system inefficiency that will become expensive during peak cooling season. Address efficiency problems before they become emergency repairs.

## System Sizing and Capacity Considerations

Arizona homes require significant cooling capacity due to extreme temperatures and high solar heat gain. The general rule suggests 1 ton of cooling capacity per 400-500 square feet, but Arizona conditions often require higher capacity calculations.

Undersized systems run continuously during extreme heat, leading to premature wear, high energy bills, and inability to maintain comfortable temperatures. Systems struggling to cool homes during moderate weather will fail completely during 107°F conditions.

Oversized systems cycle too frequently, failing to remove humidity and creating uncomfortable conditions. Proper sizing requires professional load calculations considering insulation, window placement, orientation, and local climate conditions.

Home additions, renovations, or improved insulation can change cooling requirements, making previously adequate systems insufficient for current needs. Professional load calculations determine whether your current system can handle extreme heat demands.

Ductwork sizing must match system capacity for optimal performance. Undersized ducts restrict airflow, reducing efficiency and system capacity during peak demand periods.
