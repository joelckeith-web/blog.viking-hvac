## Common Causes of AC Breakdowns in Chandler

Arizona's intense heat pushes AC systems harder than almost any other climate in the country. Understanding what typically goes wrong helps you recognize problems early and communicate effectively with your repair technician.

### Refrigerant Leaks

Refrigerant is the lifeblood of your cooling system. When levels drop due to leaks, your AC loses cooling capacity progressively — you may notice warm air from vents, ice forming on refrigerant lines, or hissing sounds near the outdoor unit. In Arizona's heat, even a small leak quickly becomes a major comfort problem as your system can't keep up with cooling demands.

### Frozen Evaporator Coils

Paradoxically, your AC can freeze even in 115-degree heat. Restricted airflow from dirty filters, blocked vents, or blower motor problems causes the evaporator coil to drop below freezing, forming ice that blocks airflow entirely. Low refrigerant levels also cause coil freezing. A frozen system provides no cooling and can damage the compressor if allowed to run.

### Electrical Failures

Frequent cycling (turning on and off rapidly), tripped breakers, or complete power loss to your AC system often indicate electrical problems. Faulty capacitors, worn contactors, loose wiring, and damaged circuit boards are common electrical failure points, especially in older systems stressed by Arizona's extreme operating conditions.

### Compressor Failure

The compressor is your AC's most critical and expensive component. When it fails, your system cannot cool at all. Warning signs include unusual noises (grinding, clunking), the outdoor unit running but not cooling, or the system refusing to start. Compressor failure in older systems usually makes replacement the more cost-effective choice over repair.

### Clogged Condensate Drain Lines

Arizona's monsoon season humidity combined with constant AC operation creates significant condensate that must drain properly. Clogged drain lines cause water backup that can trigger safety switches (shutting your system off), cause water damage to ceilings and walls, and create conditions for mold growth.

## DIY Troubleshooting Before Calling for Service

Before calling for emergency repair, check these common issues that you can resolve yourself:

### Quick Fixes to Try First

- **Check your thermostat** — Verify it's set to "cool" mode, the temperature is set below current room temperature, and batteries are fresh (if applicable)
- **Check your air filter** — A completely clogged filter can cause system shutdown. Replace it and restart your system
- **Check your circuit breaker** — Look for a tripped breaker in your electrical panel. Reset it once — if it trips again, stop and call a professional (repeated tripping indicates an electrical problem)
- **Check for ice** — If you see ice on the refrigerant lines or indoor coil, turn off the system and set the fan to "on" to allow melting. Call for service to address the underlying cause
- **Check outdoor unit** — Ensure the outdoor unit is running and not blocked by debris, furniture, or vegetation. Clear at least 2 feet of space around all sides

### When These Fixes Don't Work

If your system still isn't cooling after checking the basics, you likely have a problem requiring professional diagnosis and repair. Don't continue running a malfunctioning system — it can cause additional damage and increase repair costs.

## When to Call for Emergency AC Repair

Some situations demand immediate professional response. Call for emergency service if you experience:

### No Cooling at All

Your system is running but producing warm air, or it won't turn on at all. In Arizona's extreme heat, a home without cooling becomes dangerous within 2-4 hours during peak summer temperatures.

### Burning Smells or Smoke

**Shut off your system immediately** if you smell burning or see smoke. This indicates an electrical problem that poses a fire hazard. Do not attempt to restart the system — call for emergency service.

### Strange or Loud Noises

Grinding, screeching, or banging sounds indicate mechanical failure in progress. Continuing to run the system risks catastrophic damage to expensive components. Turn off the system and call for service.

### Water Leaks

Significant water pooling around your indoor unit suggests drainage problems, frozen coils, or component failures that can cause structural water damage if not addressed quickly.

### Dangerously High Indoor Temperatures

If indoor temperatures exceed 90°F and your system cannot bring them down, call for emergency service — especially if children, elderly family members, or people with health conditions are in the home.

### Temporary Cooling Strategies While Waiting

If you're waiting for emergency service during extreme heat:

- Close blinds and curtains to block solar heat gain
- Use portable fans to circulate air
- Stay hydrated and move to the coolest room in the house
- Avoid using heat-generating appliances (ovens, dryers)
- Consider relocating to a cooled location if temperatures become dangerous
- Place damp towels on skin for evaporative cooling
- Check on elderly neighbors who may also be affected
